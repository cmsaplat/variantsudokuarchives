Line Sudoku Variants

Have you ever seen a Sudoku grid with a bunch of squiggly lines on the screen? Green, magenta, orange, maybe some odder colours like grey? Those lines usually indicate restrictions unique to specific cells (as in the cells that they cover). However, every line has its different restrictions--we'll be covering the most common ones in this webpage.

  1. German Whispers
  2. Renbans
  3. Entropics
  4. Palindromes
  5. Region Sums


German Whispers

Classic sudoku rules apply. Additionally, every digit on a line must differ from its neighbour (on the line) by at least 5 (inclusive). Digits on a line can repeat if allowed by classic sudoku rules.
+ German Whisper lines are normally green, specifically neon green, though it may be different depending on the setter's stylistic preferences.

There are a few interesting tidbits about this variant, which are all useful for solving.

Firstly, because of the digits we're limited to (1-9, as per classic sudoku rules), the digits on the line go in an up-and-down pattern (up being above 5, down meaning below 5). This means that, once you have a number down, you can usually get a general scope of the rest of the numbers on the line.

This represents an ideal situation, where, with this information, you're able to ascribe digits to certain line cells.

Secondly, only specific digits can go on the line. You can't put a 5 on any point of a german whisper line without the rest of the line being unsolvable. 4s and 6s can only go on the ends or (region-crossing) turns of a line, because the values before and after them are forced to be the same (i.e., 949 or 161)--this would break classic sudoku rules. Renbans

Classic sudoku rules apply. Additionally, every digit on a line must be consecutive, though not necessarily placed in order.
+ Renban Lines are normally magenta, specifically neon magenta, though it may be different depending on the setter's stylistic preferences.

Renbans on higher levels have a tendency to be abstract, but for simplicity's sake, we'll be covering the strategies you'd see in a lower or intermediate level renban sudoku.

Firstly, on renban lines with 1s or 9s, it's best to think of that line as "branching out" from said digit. For example, if you had a renban line that was five cells long and the digit 1 was on it, then the digits on that line must be 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5.

Secondly, if a renban line has been given two non-consecutive digits, then all digits in between those two must be on the line.

Thirdly, if said renban is restricted to one column, row, or box, then the given numbers must be completely eliminated from the renban. This means that any consecutive string containing said number cannot be the solution to that line (note that this doesn't mean individual digits can't go on that line--just that string in particular). This usually isn't as useful as the other two above, but it's good to keep in mind, especially for cases where you may not be able to see a way forward with the first two strategies.

Entropics

Classic sudoku rules apply. Additionally, every line must contain a set with a low digit (123), medium digit (456), and high digit (789). Digits on a line can repeat if allowed by classic sudoku rules.
+ Entropic lines are normally peach-coloured, though it may be different depending on the setter's stylistic preferences.

Something odd about this variant is that its solving patterns are extremely dependent on the setter's style. However, the best place to start is by determining a lined cell's parity--that is, whether if it's a low, medium, or high digit. Think of it as marking the up-and-down of German Whisper lines.

... and unfortunately, that is where the strategy for solving entropic lines diverges. Becauuse they're so versatile, there's no common use for them--their most common application (at least in simple variant puzzles) is their low-middle-high parity being used to deduce values on the line itself.

Region Sums

Classic sudoku rules apply. Additionally, every region (i.e., 3x3 square) a line passes through must add to the same sum. Some sums must be deduced.
+Region sum lines are normally blue, though it may be different depending on the setter's stylistic preferences.

The best way to approach region sum lines is to treat every section of the line as its own pseudo-killer cage. You can use minimum/maximum killer logic on these, which helps give you some context for what you're looking for in said line.
